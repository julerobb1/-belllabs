Coaxial Cable Systems: The Underground Backbone

While microwave towers dotted the American landscape, beneath the surface lay another critical component of AT&T's Long Lines network: the extensive coaxial cable system. Beginning in the 1930s and expanding dramatically in the post-war era, these underground cables provided high-capacity, weather-resistant transmission paths that formed the most reliable segments of America's telecommunications backbone.

These cable systems represented some of the most sophisticated engineering of their time, requiring innovations in materials science, electronics, and logistics to deploy across vast distances while maintaining signal integrity.

Evolution of Coaxial Cable Systems

1936-1941: L1 System

The first commercial coaxial cable system (L1) was deployed between New York and Philadelphia, carrying 480 telephone channels or one television channel. It used large copper coaxial tubes with repeaters every 5-8 miles.

1948-1951: L3 System

The L3 system significantly increased capacity to 1,860 telephone channels or three television channels. It represented a major engineering advancement with improved amplifiers and better cable design.

1960s: L4 System

The L4 system further increased capacity to 3,600 telephone channels. It used smaller diameter cables and transistorized repeaters, making it more economical and reliable than previous systems.

1970s: L5 System

The L5 system represented the pinnacle of coaxial technology, carrying up to 10,800 telephone conversations on a single coaxial pair. It employed sophisticated multiplexing techniques and advanced solid-state repeaters.

Technical Design and Implementation

Coaxial cable systems were engineering marvels that required precise manufacturing and installation processes to function properly across thousands of miles.

Cable Construction

Each cable contained multiple coaxial tubes with a center copper conductor surrounded by polyethylene insulation and an outer copper shield. Later systems used aluminum shields to reduce weight and cost. The entire assembly was wrapped in waterproof layers and armored for protection against environmental damage.

Repeater Stations

Signal amplification occurred at repeater stations spaced 4-8 miles apart, depending on the system generation. These stations housed sophisticated electronic equipment that regenerated the attenuated signals without introducing distortion. Later systems featured automatic equalization to compensate for temperature and aging effects.

Power Systems

Repeaters received power through the cable itself, with power feed stations typically spaced every 50-150 miles. This constant-current power system was designed with multiple redundancies to ensure continuous operation, even during regional power outages.

Multiplexing

To maximize capacity, these systems employed frequency-division multiplexing (FDM), which divided the available bandwidth into multiple channels. By the 1970s, a single L5 coaxial system could carry over 10,000 simultaneous telephone conversations using a complex hierarchy of multiplexing groups.

National Deployment

The deployment of AT&T's coaxial cable network represented one of the largest infrastructure projects in American telecommunications history.

  • Transcontinental L3 - In 1951, AT&T completed the first coast-to-coast coaxial cable system, capable of carrying television signals across the nation.
  • North-South Routes - Major cable routes connected Chicago to New Orleans, New York to Miami, and Seattle to San Diego, completing a national grid.
  • Urban Connections - Dense metropolitan areas were served by multiple redundant coaxial paths to ensure reliable service.
  • International Links - Submarine coaxial cables extended the network to international destinations, starting with the 1956 TAT-1 transatlantic telephone cable.

By the mid-1970s, AT&T had deployed over 60,000 route miles of coaxial cable systems across the United States, forming an intricate underground network that complemented its microwave tower system.

Engineering Challenges

The construction of the coaxial cable network presented numerous engineering challenges:

  • Route Selection - Engineers had to carefully select routes that balanced directness with favorable terrain while securing countless right-of-way agreements with landowners.
  • Cable Laying - Special plows and trenching machines were developed to bury cables at consistent depths while maintaining precise handling standards.
  • River Crossings - Major waterways required specialized techniques, including directional drilling, suspension systems, or weighted submarine sections.
  • Signal Integrity - Maintaining signal quality over thousands of miles required innovative amplifier designs and sophisticated equalization systems.
  • Lightning Protection - The entire system required comprehensive protection against lightning strikes and ground potential differences.

Legacy and Transition

The coaxial cable era began its decline in the 1980s as fiber optic technology proved superior in capacity, distance capabilities, and maintenance costs. However, the engineering principles developed for coaxial systems laid the groundwork for modern telecommunications infrastructure.

Many coaxial routes were eventually replaced with fiber optic cables, often using the same rights-of-way and conduits. Some sections remain in service for local distribution or as backup systems. The vast underground network of manholes, ducts, and equipment buildings constructed for the coaxial network continues to serve as vital infrastructure for today's communications systems.

The techniques developed for long-distance coaxial systems influenced modern cable television networks, broadband internet infrastructure, and undersea cable systems that circle the globe today.
