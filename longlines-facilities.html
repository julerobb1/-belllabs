AT&T Long Lines Facilities

The AT&T Long Lines network was built on an extensive infrastructure of remarkable facilities across the United States. These architectural and engineering marvels formed the backbone of America's telecommunications system for much of the 20th century.

Types of Long Lines Facilities

Microwave Relay Towers

These distinctive towers, often featuring horn-shaped antennas, formed the backbone of the network from the 1950s to the 1980s. Spaced approximately 30 miles apart, they relayed telephone conversations, television broadcasts, and data across line-of-sight paths.

Many towers featured concrete bunkers designed to withstand nuclear attack, reflecting their critical infrastructure status during the Cold War era.

View microwave tower gallery →

Coaxial Cable Repeater Stations

Before microwave systems and alongside them, AT&T buried massive coaxial cable systems alongside roads and railway lines. These underground cables required repeater stations every few miles to amplify signals.

Many of these utilitarian buildings dot the American landscape, often identifiable by their windowless design and AT&T Long Lines emblems.

View repeater station locations →

Switching Centers

These large facilities housed the complex electromechanical and later electronic equipment that routed calls across the network. Major switching centers were often impressive architectural statements in urban centers.

Many contained hardened areas designed to withstand disasters, housing critical telecommunications equipment for national security purposes.

Explore major switching centers →

Earth Stations

With the advent of satellite communications, AT&T built impressive earth stations featuring massive parabolic antennas. These facilities communicated with satellites like Telstar, enabling intercontinental telecommunications.

Notable examples include the Andover Earth Station in Maine, which communicated with the first Telstar satellite in 1962.

Preservation Status

Many Long Lines facilities have been decommissioned as technology evolved beyond the need for this physical infrastructure. Their fates include:

Repurposed

Some facilities have found new life as data centers, cell towers, office spaces, or even private residences. Their robust construction and often remote locations make them suitable for various reuses.

Abandoned

Many remote facilities stand abandoned, slowly deteriorating but still visible on the landscape. These telecommunications ruins are of interest to industrial archaeology enthusiasts and photographers.

Demolished

Some structures, particularly in urban areas, have been demolished to make way for new development. Documentation of these lost sites remains important for historical records.

Preserved

A small number of facilities have been preserved for their historical significance. Some have been listed on the National Register of Historic Places, recognizing their importance to American technological history.
